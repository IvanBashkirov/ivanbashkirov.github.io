The Public Service Hall in Tbilisi is a building that looks like a cluster of mushrooms landed on the riverbank and decided to start issuing documents. Inside, it is the most efficient government building I have ever stood in. I went in one morning in February with a passport and a Georgian address, and I came out before lunch as a registered business. Nobody asked about my vision. There is no field on the form for a vision.

The regime I registered under is for individual entrepreneurs with what Georgia calls Small Business Status. The deal is short: you pay one percent of turnover, declared monthly, up to a ceiling of 500,000 lari a year.[^1] Not profit. Turnover. The state is not interested in the story of your costs. Money comes in, a hundredth of it goes out, everyone moves on. The declaration is filed online and takes about as long as making coffee. In exchange you agree to stay small and to not do certain things, such as run a currency exchange, which I can live with.

I want to be careful here, because posts about Georgia's one percent tax tend to be written by men optimising their lives from a co-working space, and they tend to have affiliate links. This is not that. I moved here for reasons that had more to do with a piano, a budget and a city I liked than with tax. The regime was a discovery, not a destination. But it has done something to my head that I did not expect, and that is what this document is about.

First, the entity itself, in full:

| Parameter | Value |
| --- | --- |
| Legal form | Individual entrepreneur |
| Headcount | 1 |
| Tax on turnover | 1% |
| Office | kitchen table, north-facing |
| Board meetings held | 0 |
| All-hands attendance | 100% |

The word "founder" was not built for this table. The word implies co-founders, a cap table, a narrative arc that ends on a stage. When I say I am a founder at a dinner in Tbilisi, the next question is "how many people," and when I answer "one," there is a polite recalibration, as if I had claimed to own an airline and then clarified that it is a kite.

I have come to think the recalibration runs the wrong way. Strip the word down and a founder is a person who causes a thing to exist and is answerable for whether it survives. By that definition the one-person version is not the diminished form. It is the control condition. There is no team to absorb the blame, no market narrative that can be maintained internally after it has failed externally. The experiment has one variable and it is me.

> A company is a story you tell other people. A turnover declaration is a story you tell the tax office, and it has to be true.

That is the real effect of the one percent regime, and it is psychological before it is financial. The declaration is monthly. Every month I type a number into a government website, and the number is the total of what strangers paid me for things I made. There is no line for effort. There is no line for how promising the roadmap looks, how the vision is compounding, or how many hours were worked. The form accepts revenue and nothing else. In months where nothing chargeable shipped, I type a zero, and the website accepts my zero without comment, which is somehow worse than a rejection would be.

I have registered businesses in two other countries, so I have a baseline. In London I once opened a limited company that existed mostly to invoice, and even that came with a shadow organisation of confirmation statements, corporation tax computations and an accountant who emailed in a tone of permanent mild disappointment. In Berlin I did not get as far as a company; I got as far as the Anmeldung, which is the German ritual where you queue to inform the state that you exist, and the state schedules a follow-up. Against this backdrop, a tax regime that consists of one number and one percent sign reads less like policy and more like an editor. It has cut everything from the definition of "business" except the part where somebody pays you.

Hustle culture and the one percent form are natural enemies. Hustle culture is a genre of performance: the 5 a.m. photograph, the thread about relentlessness, the grind as content. The form does not watch the performance. You cannot declare a morning routine. But the form is equally hard on the opposite camp, the one where "lifestyle business" is pronounced as a diagnosis. The form taxes a freelance illustrator and me identically and files us under the same term, which in Georgian paperwork is not "founder" but something closer to "physical person conducting economic activity." I have started to prefer it. It is accurate on all three counts.

What the regime rewards, mechanically, is shipping things that charge money. Not growth theatre, not stealth, not the roadmap. A one-person company has no internal audience to perform progress for. There are no standups to survive, no manager to demo to, no all-hands to hold a slide up in front of. The only external checkpoint left standing is the invoice, and the invoice only exists if the thing shipped. So the tax form has quietly become my planning system. One objective, one key result, reviewed monthly by the Revenue Service of Georgia.

There are costs to a headcount of one, and I log them here for honesty. Every skill I lack is a production outage. When I am sick, the company is sick. Nobody argues with my bad ideas until a customer does, at which point the argument is expensive. And there is no one to tell the good news to at the moment it happens, which sounds like a small thing until a stranger pays you nine dollars at eleven at night and the only colleague available for the announcement is the kettle.

Still. Somewhere along the way "founder" became a costume, and the costume came with obligations: raise, scale, hire, exit. The Georgian form suggests a quieter definition, and I am adopting it. Cause things to exist. Charge money for them. Declare it. Pay the one percent. Repeat until the numbers say something, in either direction.

The company is one person in Tbilisi. The company held its annual general meeting this morning at the kitchen table, and the minutes read: ship more.

[^1]: Above 500,000 GEL of annual turnover the rate rises to 3%, and somewhere beyond that you presumably graduate into being a real company, with an accountant and opinions about transfer pricing. This is not currently a risk.
