---
title: "Charging Money Before It's Ready"
ref: "047"
date: 2026-07-14
kind: essay
standfirst: "Autosampler v0.1 was not ready. The payment button worked anyway."
---

Autosampler v0.1 shipped today. It takes a folder of audio stems and outputs a labelled sample pack: sliced at the onsets, sorted into drums, bass and other, named honestly, zipped with a manifest. That is the whole product. It costs nine dollars.

It ships with fourteen known issues. I know it is fourteen because they are numbered in a file called KNOWN_ISSUES.txt that is included in the download, which my past self would have considered an act of commercial suicide. Pitch detection believes a suspicious number of things are E. Anything with reverb on the tail gets sliced with what I can only describe as optimism. If you feed it a full mix instead of stems, it labels the entire song "other," which is technically correct and practically useless. The progress bar is decorative.

By any standard I held a year ago, this is not ready. And a year ago, that standard is exactly what I would have obeyed. I would have fixed the fourteen issues, discovered eleven more in the process, rewritten the slicer, redesigned the landing page twice, and arrived, eight months later, at a more polished product with the same number of customers I had on day one: zero. I know this because it is a summary of my previous project. GameTok was polished. GameTok is a one-line entry on a company register now.

So this time the price went on before the polish. Not because nine dollars makes it good, but because nine dollars makes the question answerable.

A free tool collects compliments. I had given early builds of Autosampler to a handful of producers, and the feedback was warm and completely useless. "Cool idea." "Nice, I'd use this." These sentences cost the sender nothing, and their information content is priced accordingly. The moment there is a number on the page, the sentences change species. Nobody says "cool idea" to a price. They either pay it, or they tell you exactly what is stopping them, and both of those are data.

> "Ready" is a feeling. A price is a fact. Only one of them can be tested.

I had rehearsed the arguments against, and they are real. Charging for something unfinished feels disrespectful to the customer. It exposes the flaws to people with a receipt and therefore standing to complain. And it converts embarrassment from a private feeling into a business risk. What I had not appreciated is that this conversion is the entire point. Unpaid embarrassment is inert; it just accumulates. Paid embarrassment has a queue, a severity ranking and a service level. A customer turns shame into a to-do list.

The landing page took an afternoon and went through one major revision: I deleted the second paragraph. The first paragraph said what the tool does. The second paragraph said what the tool would eventually become, and every sentence in it was a small loan taken out against future work. One paragraph, one price, one button. Under the button, in plain text: "v0.1. Fourteen known issues, listed in the download. Refund if it wastes your time." That last sentence did more for my nerves than any amount of QA.

Then, last night at around eleven, the notification. Nine dollars, minus fees, from someone whose name I do not recognise, in a country I have not been to. No note. No reply to the receipt. Just a stranger, somewhere, who looked at an unfinished tool made by an individual entrepreneur at a kitchen table in Tbilisi, and decided the price of a sandwich was worth the bet.

I want to record, with clinical precision, what that did, because the amount is irrelevant and the effect was not. Every prior signal this project had received was social: friends being kind, producers being polite, my own conviction cycling on a roughly 48-hour period. This was the first signal that could not be attributed to kindness. Strangers do not pay to spare your feelings. The nine dollars is the least ambiguous data point I have collected in a year and a half, and I have run analytics dashboards with forty charts on them.

It also, immediately, changed the work. This morning three of the fourteen issues got fixed, not because a roadmap said so, but because there is now a specific person who might hit them. The abstraction "users" never once got me out of bed. One actual customer, unnamed and silent, apparently does. I did not know this about myself, and I could not have learned it for free.

None of this is an argument for shipping garbage. The tool works; the fourteen issues are edges, not the middle. The bar I used was narrow: it does the one thing the paragraph says, on the input the paragraph specifies, and when it fails, it fails honestly. That bar is much lower than "ready" and much higher than "demo," and I suspect it is where most useful software actually lives.

Version 0.2 will have eleven known issues. The price stays. The stranger, wherever they are, gets it free.
