Two weeks ago I filed an application to strike GameTok off the company register. The form took eleven minutes, most of which I spent looking for the registration number, which I had written down somewhere safe in the way that guarantees a thing is never found again. There is a modest fee. There is a waiting period in case anyone objects. Nobody objected. That was, in a way, the whole story of GameTok restated one final time in legal form: nobody objected, because nobody noticed.

GameTok was a short-form video app for game discovery. Vertical feed, gameplay clips, swipe up for the next game, tap to wishlist. The pitch fit in one sentence and everyone I told it to nodded. "TikTok for finding your next game." I now know that the nod is not a signal. People nod at coherent sentences. Coherence is not demand.

I spent about a year on it. The first three months were the build, which went well, because building is the part I know how to do. Then the launch, which technically also went well, in that the app appeared on the App Store on the day I said it would and did not crash. Then nine months of what I logged at the time as "iteration" and can now identify, with the clarity of hindsight and a strike-off receipt, as four consecutive pivots. Clips from streamers, then clips from developers, then a version aimed at indie publishers, then a version aimed at whoever was left. Each pivot was reasonable. Each was supported by feedback from the handful of users we had. None of them addressed the actual problem, which was that the people who want to discover games already have a way of doing it, and it is called their friends.

The final numbers, for the record, because a post-mortem without numbers is a eulogy: 212 downloads. Three weekly active users at the end, two of whom were my own test devices. Total revenue: zero, by design at first and then by momentum. One year.

So much for the launch. Now the part I actually want to write about, which is the ending, because the ending taught me things the launch had no way to teach.

**A company is a list of obligations, not a list of features.** On launch day I thought of GameTok as a codebase with an icon. The strike-off application forces the true inventory: the Apple developer account, the analytics subscription, the transactional email plan, the domain renewals, a trademark application I had filed in an optimistic March, the data I was holding about the 212 people and my duty to delete it properly. The product turned out to be maybe a third of the company by mass. Nobody tells you this at launch, because at launch there is nothing to wind down. You learn what a company is made of by taking one apart, the same way you learn what a piano is made of, and with a similar feeling of it being too late to matter.

**Winding down produces the only honest usage data you will ever collect.** I emailed the remaining users to say the app was closing and their data would be deleted. This is the one message in the app's history with a guaranteed open rate among people who care, and the responses tell you exactly what you had. I received two replies. One was kind. One was an out-of-office. There is a version of this story where the shutdown email triggers a wave of protest and you discover, too late, that you had something. I mention this version because it is the one founders privately expect, and its absence is informative. The silence is not an insult. It is the metric.

> The strike-off form has no field for how hard you tried. It asks whether the company has assets, whether it has debts, and whether anyone objects. Effort is not an asset.

**The paperwork is indifferent, and the indifference is useful.** Every artefact from the launch era was designed to be persuasive: the store listing, the deck, the copy, even the changelog had adjectives in it. The dissolution paperwork is the first document in the company's life that has no interest in persuading anyone of anything. Filling it in is the closest thing the process offers to an exit interview, conducted by a form. Assets: none. Liabilities: none. Employees: not applicable. It is remarkable how quickly a form like that deflates a year of narrative. I recommend the experience in the specific sense that I recommend cold water.

**Killing it is a shipping act.** This is the part I keep coming back to. The decision to stop took longer than it should have, because the sunk year sat on the scale and I kept counting it as an asset. But once decided, the wind-down was a project like any other, with a scope and a deadline, and I ran it like one. Users notified. Data deleted, verifiably. App delisted. Accounts closed in dependency order. Register application filed. The last thing GameTok shipped was its own ending, on time and under budget, which is more than I can say for two of its features.

What would I tell the version of me from a year ago? Not "don't build it." He would not have listened, and anyway the build was the cheapest tuition on this list. I would tell him to name one specific person who has the problem, and to notice that he could not. I do not watch short-form video. I read reviews and ask friends. I built a discovery feed for a species of person I had read about but never met, and I mistook the coherence of the pitch for the existence of the customer.

The register now lists GameTok as struck off. The entry is one line. It sits in a public database next to thousands of other one-line endings, which I find neither sad nor encouraging, just accurate. If you are going to fail, fail with clean books. Grief is administratively simple when it is documented, and the eleven minutes of paperwork were the most instructive of the year.
