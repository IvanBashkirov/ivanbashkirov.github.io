Georgia taxes my turnover at one percent, which is less a tax policy than the removal of my last respectable excuse. When the state takes almost nothing, the remaining overhead is you.

I want to explain what that sentence has done to my head over the six weeks since I registered, because it has done more than any productivity system I have ever bought, and it cost one percent.

## The Public Service Hall

The Public Service Hall in Tbilisi is a building that looks like a cluster of mushrooms landed on the riverbank and decided to start issuing documents. Inside, it is the most efficient government building I have ever stood in. I went in one morning in May with a passport and a Georgian address — while GameTok was still officially alive and unofficially dead, though I had not yet filed the paperwork admitting either — and I came out before lunch as a registered business. Nobody asked about my vision. There is no field on the form for a vision.

The regime I registered under is for individual entrepreneurs with what Georgia calls Small Business Status. The deal is short: you pay one percent of turnover, declared monthly, up to a ceiling of 500,000 lari a year.[^1] Not profit. Turnover. The state is not interested in the story of your costs. Money comes in, a hundredth of it goes out, everyone moves on. The declaration is filed online and takes about as long as making coffee. In exchange you agree to stay small and to not do certain things, such as run a currency exchange, which I can live with.

I want to be careful here, because posts about Georgia's one percent tax tend to be written by men optimising their lives from a co-working space, and they tend to have affiliate links. This is not that. I moved here for reasons that had more to do with a piano, a budget and a city I liked than with tax. The regime was a discovery, not a destination. But it has restructured my thinking in ways I did not see coming, and that is what this document is about.

First, the entity itself, in full:

| Parameter | Value |
| --- | --- |
| Legal form | Individual entrepreneur |
| Headcount | 1 |
| Tax on turnover | 1% |
| Office | kitchen table, north-facing |
| Board meetings held | 0 |
| All-hands attendance | 100% |

## The indie maths

Here is the arithmetic that the regime exposes, and I encourage you to run your own version, because the exercise is clarifying in the way cold water is clarifying.

My operation costs, all in, about $180 a month to exist: hosting for this site and PAWND.IO, the Apple developer fee amortised monthly, a code-signing certificate, one analytics service on the cheapest tier, and a domain habit I am managing. My life in Tbilisi — rent, food, the session on Tuesdays, strings and reeds and the occasional irresponsible purchase of sheet music — costs about $1,700 a month. Call the total $1,900. Under this regime, turnover of $1,920 a month covers everything, tax included, because the tax on $1,920 is $19.20.

Now run the same number in the cities I came from. In London, clearing £1,600 a month after tax, National Insurance and a rent that assumes two incomes requires a gross salary somewhere north of £30,000, which requires a job, which requires the forty hours that were supposed to be spent on the products. The standard indie path in London is therefore a savings runway: eighteen months of burn in an account, a countdown timer, and a genre of anxiety that compounds monthly as the runway shortens. I know the genre. I ran that countdown in my head for years and always found the number too frightening to start.

In Tbilisi under the one percent regime, the equation inverts. The question stops being "how long can I survive without revenue" and becomes "what is the smallest revenue that makes this permanent." And the answer is embarrassingly small. MacNoSleep, a menu-bar utility that stops Macs falling asleep, sells for a few dollars and covers the entire infrastructure bill in a good week. Nineteen hundred dollars a month is 380 sales of a five-dollar utility, or 63 subscriptions at $30, or — to use a number I am currently staring at — three fitness creators paying a real platform fee. These are not startup numbers. No venture capitalist would cross the street for them. That is precisely what makes them reachable, and reachable numbers change behaviour in a way that inspiring ones never did.

There is a second-order effect of the small break-even that took me longer to notice. When the target is eighteen months of runway, every product idea gets evaluated against a venture-shaped question: could this be enormous. Ideas that could merely be good — a utility, a niche tool for four hundred hypertrophy clients, a sample-pack robot for producers — fail that question automatically and never get built. When the target is nineteen hundred dollars a month, the same ideas pass, and the portfolio changes shape entirely. Small ideas ship faster, fail cheaper, and compound. The regime did not just lower my costs. It lowered the minimum viable ambition, and the minimum viable ambition was the thing that had been stopping me.

I filed my first declaration on June 19th. The number in it was three figures, all of it from a utility that keeps laptops awake. The state's share would not cover a decent lunch. I have rarely felt more like a real business than I did typing that absurd little number into a government website, because the number was true, and every number I had typed into a pitch deck was a forecast wearing a suit.

## The removal of excuses

That is the mechanical part. The psychological part is bigger.

Every founder carries a portfolio of respectable excuses, and the respectable ones are the dangerous ones, because nobody challenges them at dinner. The corporate structure needs setting up first. The accounting is complicated. The tax position needs advice. I have held all of these, sincerely, in two other countries. In London I once opened a limited company that existed mostly to invoice, and even that came with a shadow organisation of confirmation statements, corporation tax computations and an accountant who emailed in a tone of permanent mild disappointment. In Berlin I did not get as far as a company; I got as far as the Anmeldung, which is the German ritual where you queue to inform the state that you exist, and the state schedules a follow-up. In both cities, "the admin" was a real cost, and therefore available as a reason, and a reason available is a reason used.

Georgia has confiscated the excuse. The entire administrative surface of my company is one login and one number a month. There is no accountant to wait for, no structure to optimise, no advice to seek before starting. When the whole apparatus of business-having takes four minutes a month, the question "why isn't this shipping" has exactly one place left to look, and it is not the paperwork. The regime does not motivate you. It removes the furniture you were hiding behind, which resembles motivation from a distance.

The monthly declaration enforces this with a cruelty I have come to respect. Every month I type a number into a government website, and the number is the total of what strangers paid me for things I made. There is no line for effort. There is no line for how promising the roadmap looks, how the vision is compounding, or how many hours were worked. The form accepts revenue and nothing else. In a month where nothing chargeable ships, I will type a zero, and the website will accept my zero without comment, which is somehow worse than a rejection would be.

> A company is a story you tell other people. A turnover declaration is a story you tell the tax office, and it has to be true.

## What "founder" means at headcount one

The word "founder" was not built for the table above. The word implies co-founders, a cap table, a narrative arc that ends on a stage. When I say I am a founder at a dinner in Tbilisi, the next question is "how many people," and when I answer "one," there is a polite recalibration, as if I had claimed to own an airline and then clarified that it is a kite.

I have come to think the recalibration runs the wrong way. Strip the word down and a founder is a person who causes a thing to exist and is answerable for whether it survives. By that definition the one-person version is not the diminished form. It is the control condition. There is no team to absorb the blame, no market narrative that can be maintained internally after it has failed externally. The experiment has one variable and it is me.

Hustle culture and the one percent form are natural enemies. Hustle culture is a genre of performance: the 5 a.m. photograph, the thread about relentlessness, the grind as content. The form does not watch the performance. You cannot declare a morning routine. But the form is equally hard on the opposite camp, the one where "lifestyle business" is pronounced as a diagnosis. The form taxes a freelance illustrator and me identically and files us under the same term, which in Georgian paperwork is not "founder" but something closer to "physical person conducting economic activity." I have started to prefer it. It is accurate on all three counts.

What the regime rewards, mechanically, is shipping things that charge money. Not growth theatre, not stealth, not the roadmap. A one-person company has no internal audience to perform progress for. There are no standups to survive, no manager to demo to, no all-hands to hold a slide up in front of. The only external checkpoint left standing is the invoice, and the invoice only exists if the thing shipped. So the tax form has quietly become my planning system. One objective, one key result, reviewed monthly by the Revenue Service of Georgia.

## Pre-sales, or the form applied earlier

The June declaration also contained, by omission, the most expensive lesson of my year. What it did not contain was any revenue from GameTok, because GameTok never charged anyone anything, and eleven days before I filed that declaration I had filed a different form, the one that strikes GameTok's company off the register. Six months of my best engineering appears in the state's records twice: once as a zero, once as a dissolution. The Revenue Service of Georgia has, without meaning to, written the most concise post-mortem available.

GameTok followed the sequence I was trained on: build the product, launch the product, then discover whether anyone wanted the product. The discovery phase is placed at the end, where it is most expensive. The one percent form has been teaching me, monthly, that only revenue is admissible evidence — and if only revenue is admissible, the rational move is to demand that evidence before the build, not after. This is not an original thought. Every book on customer development says it. I had read the books. The books did not survive contact with my enthusiasm for building; the strike-off receipt apparently did.

So the next project has the discovery phase bolted to the front, structurally, where my enthusiasm cannot unbolt it. ChallengeClub — a monetisation platform for gym and fitness creators — sits behind a gate: three paid commitments before I write a single line of production code. Not letters of intent, not waitlist signups, not warm nods. Money, or a signed order with money attached, three times, from three different creators. I have run six discovery calls in the past three weeks. The count of commitments currently stands at zero, which means the count of production code also stands at zero, and I want to record how strange and correct that feels. Under the old sequence I would be four weeks into the payments infrastructure by now, building admin panels for gym owners I had never spoken to.

The gate is the declaration form, applied earlier. Both are the same instrument: a place where only the number counts, positioned so that I cannot narrate my way past it. The tax office runs one for me at the end of every month. The pre-sales gate I have to run for myself, which is harder, because I am on both sides of the counter and one of me is very persuasive.

## The costs, for honesty

There are costs to a headcount of one, and I log them here because this document would be a brochure without them. Every skill I lack is a production outage. When I am sick, the company is sick. Nobody argues with my bad ideas until a customer does, at which point the argument is expensive — GameTok being the standing exhibit. Decisions have no second reader. The wins have no witness, and it turns out a win without a witness has a half-life of about an hour, after which it converts into the anxiety of the next task. The kettle has heard a lot of announcements this year.

And the regime itself deserves one caveat: one percent of turnover is only generous if you have margin. A hardware business running thin margins could be worse off here than under a profit tax. The regime is built for people like me, whose cost of goods is electricity and stubbornness. If your business ships atoms, run the maths before you book the flight.

## The quieter definition

Still. Somewhere along the way "founder" became a costume, and the costume came with obligations: raise, scale, hire, exit, narrate. The Georgian form suggests a quieter definition, and I am adopting it. Cause things to exist. Charge money for them. Declare it. Pay the one percent. Repeat until the numbers say something, in either direction — and when they say it, believe them the first time, because I now know precisely what it costs to ask for the answer fourteen different ways.

The company is one person in Tbilisi. The company held its annual general meeting this morning at the kitchen table, and the minutes read: ship more.

[^1]: Above 500,000 GEL of annual turnover the rate rises to 3%, and somewhere beyond that you presumably graduate into being a real company, with an accountant and opinions about transfer pricing. This is not currently a risk.
